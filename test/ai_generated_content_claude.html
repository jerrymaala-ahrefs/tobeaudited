Book Report: Are You There God? It's Me, Margaret

by Judy Blume

Introduction and Overview

Judy Blume's Are You There God? It's Me, Margaret, published in 1970, stands as one of the most influential young adult novels of the twentieth century. This groundbreaking work addresses the complex experiences of adolescence through the eyes of Margaret Simon, an eleven-year-old girl navigating the turbulent waters of pre-teen life. The novel's enduring relevance stems from its honest portrayal of puberty, religious identity, and social pressures that continue to resonate with readers across generations. Blume's willingness to tackle subjects previously considered taboo in children's literature established her as a pioneer in realistic fiction for young readers.

Plot Summary and Setting

The story follows Margaret Simon as her family relocates from New York City to suburban New Jersey, forcing her to leave behind her beloved grandmother and familiar urban environment. Margaret finds herself in a new school, attempting to forge friendships while grappling with questions about her religious identity—her mother is Christian, her father is Jewish, and her parents have chosen to raise her without formal religion. Throughout the school year, Margaret joins a secret club with her new friends Nancy, Gretchen, and Janie, where they discuss boys, bras, and their developing bodies. The narrative unfolds through Margaret's personal conversations with God, whom she addresses directly despite her lack of formal religious affiliation, creating an intimate window into her innermost thoughts and fears.

Character Development and Margaret's Journey

Margaret emerges as a remarkably authentic protagonist whose voice captures the uncertainty and excitement of early adolescence with striking honesty. Her character development throughout the novel reflects the universal struggle of finding one's identity while balancing parental expectations, peer pressure, and personal desires. Margaret's relationship with her parents reveals the complexities of family dynamics, particularly as she discovers hidden tensions between her parents and paternal grandparents regarding religion. Her friendship with Nancy Wheeler introduces her to new social dynamics and the sometimes cruel hierarchies of pre-teen girl culture. Through her experiences with her teacher Mr. Benedict's year-long assignment to explore religion, Margaret demonstrates intellectual curiosity and emotional maturity beyond her years.

Themes of Religious Identity

The exploration of religious identity forms a central pillar of the novel's thematic structure, presenting religion not as doctrine but as a personal journey of discovery. Margaret's unique position between Christianity and Judaism allows Blume to examine faith from an outsider's perspective, questioning traditional assumptions about religious belonging. Her visits to different houses of worship, including a synagogue with her grandmother and various Christian churches with her friends, showcase the diversity of religious experience while highlighting the common human need for spiritual connection. The tension between Margaret's grandparents over her religious upbringing reveals how adult conflicts can impact children's sense of identity and belonging. Through Margaret's informal prayers to God, Blume presents spirituality as something personal and immediate rather than institutional and formal.

Coming of Age and Physical Development

The novel's frank discussion of puberty and physical development revolutionized young adult literature by acknowledging the reality of adolescent concerns about bodily changes. Margaret and her friends' preoccupation with getting their first periods and developing breasts reflects genuine pre-teen anxieties that had rarely been addressed so directly in literature for this age group. The "We Must Increase Our Bust" exercises the girls perform together transform potentially embarrassing topics into moments of friendship and shared experience. Blume handles these sensitive subjects with humor and empathy, validating young readers' concerns while providing reassurance that such feelings are normal and universal. The novel's treatment of these topics helps demystify puberty and reduces the shame and secrecy that often surrounds adolescent development.

Friendship and Social Dynamics

The portrayal of female friendship in the novel captures both the intensity and volatility of pre-teen relationships with remarkable accuracy. The Pre-Teen Sensations (PTS) club serves as a microcosm of adolescent social structures, complete with rules, secrets, and shifting loyalties that characterize this developmental stage. Nancy's role as the group's leader demonstrates how peer pressure operates within friendship circles, sometimes pushing individuals to act against their better judgment. The incident with Laura Danker, whom the girls ostracize based on rumors about her development and behavior, illustrates how quickly young people can turn to cruelty when faced with difference or perceived maturity. Margaret's eventual recognition of her participation in this bullying marks an important step in her moral development.

Family Relationships and Generational Conflicts

The complex web of family relationships in the novel extends beyond the nuclear family to explore intergenerational conflicts and their impact on children. Margaret's close relationship with her paternal grandmother, Sylvia, provides emotional support and connection to her Jewish heritage, while the estrangement from her maternal grandparents creates a void in her understanding of her Christian background. The revelation of the family rift caused by her parents' interfaith marriage adds depth to the religious identity theme while showing how adult decisions reverberate through generations. Her parents' attempts to shield Margaret from religious pressure while simultaneously dealing with their own unresolved conflicts about faith and family create a realistic portrait of well-meaning but imperfect parenting.

Literary Significance and Writing Style

Blume's writing style, characterized by its conversational tone and emotional authenticity, helped establish a new standard for young adult literature that prioritizes honest communication with readers over didactic messaging. The use of Margaret's prayers as a narrative device creates intimacy while avoiding the pitfalls of excessive internal monologue, allowing readers direct access to the protagonist's thoughts and feelings. The novel's episodic structure mirrors the rhythms of the school year, providing a familiar framework that grounds the emotional turbulence of adolescence in recognizable routines and milestones. Blume's ability to address serious topics while maintaining appropriate humor demonstrates respect for her young readers' intelligence and emotional complexity.

Cultural Impact and Controversy

Since its publication, the novel has faced numerous challenges and banning attempts due to its frank discussion of puberty and questioning of religious authority, yet these controversies have only underscored its importance in young adult literature. The book's presence on banned book lists reflects ongoing cultural anxieties about adolescent sexuality and religious diversity, while its continued popularity demonstrates readers' hunger for honest representation of their experiences. The novel's influence extends beyond literature into broader conversations about age-appropriate education, religious tolerance, and the rights of young people to access information about their developing bodies and minds. Its enduring presence in school libraries and curricula, despite persistent challenges, testifies to educators' and librarians' recognition of its value in supporting young readers through a challenging developmental period.

Conclusion and Lasting Relevance

Are You There God? It's Me, Margaret remains a touchstone of young adult literature precisely because it treats its young readers with respect and honesty, acknowledging their concerns as valid and important rather than dismissing them as trivial. The novel's exploration of identity formation, both religious and personal, continues to resonate in an increasingly diverse and complex world where young people must navigate multiple cultural influences and expectations. Blume's achievement lies not only in breaking taboos around discussing adolescent development but in creating a protagonist whose vulnerability and strength provide a model for self-acceptance and personal growth. The novel's lasting impact can be measured not only in its continued readership but in the countless books it inspired that similarly trust young readers with honest portrayals of adolescent experience. As contemporary young people continue to grapple with questions of identity, belonging, and physical development, Margaret's story offers both comfort and companionship, reminding readers that their struggles are neither new nor insurmountable.
